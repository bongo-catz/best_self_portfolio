What Is This?

Overview

This blog was written in response to an assignment "Best Self Portfolio" from the Leadership Theory course (660.320) taught at Johns Hopkins University. In this assignment, we were tasked to include three sections, as outlined below.

Interviews

The pre-work for this assignment included interviewing two individuals close to us about a moment they remembered us as our "best moment". I decided to interview my high school and middle school biology teacher Mrs. Song and my girlfriend Wenyu to see how I might have changed as a person before or after I entered University. I summarize the discussions in the section under "Interviews".

Self-Reflection

We were asked to take two self-assessments (listed and linked below) and reflect on the results of the two assessments. We were also asked to reflect upon a leadership concept we have covered in class and provide examples of when we have demonstrated this concept. I decided to focus on Transformational Leadership as that was a very recently covered concept! I summarize this reflection in the section "Self-Reflection".

Interviews

I had the pleasure of interviewing two people about a time when I was at my best self. Wanting to see how I changed from high school to university, I decided to interview one person from before and another person from after I entered University.

Mrs. Song

Name: Mrs. Song
Current Occupation: Current High School Biology Teacher at Whitney High School
Favorite Song: "Can't Help Falling in Love" by Elvis Presley

Throughout my time in high school, I knew Mrs. Song well, having first met her in 7th grade. Physically, I didn't change much over the years, but Mrs. Song never failed to notice the personal growth I underwent. From a shy yet studious student who consistently aced Kahoot quizzes, I matured into a sociable and people-oriented leader. In my sophomore year, I became involved in Mrs. Song's effort to establish a Science Olympiad program in our school. For three years, we worked tirelessly to build and hone our team.

In my senior year, Mrs. Song and I were determined to see our team advance to the States Competition. Unfortunately, on the day of the Regionals Competition, everything seemed to go wrong. Our bus arrived late, and then we were stuck in traffic. We even missed out on competing in two events. Despite the setbacks, I maintained a positive attitude and encouraged my teammates to keep going. I reminded them how much we had practiced together and tried to lift their spirits by emphasizing the importance of having fun.

After six grueling hours of competition, we finally made it to the awards ceremony. As they announced the placements of the teams, I watched anxiously, hoping for the best. Though as 6th through 3rd place were announced, I saw Mrs. Song shaking her head in disappointment. But I refused to give up hope and exclaimed “Hey, there are still 2 places left.” And then it happened - our team was declared the second-place winner!

Mrs. Song recounted this story to share that I was at my best when I utilized my optimism to help lift my teammates up, even when everything seemed to go wrong. When I turned to my team during the awards ceremony and said that there were still 2 places left, Mrs. Song felt it showed how much I believed in the people I cared for. While others were becoming discouraged again, I still maintained hope. A quote from Mrs. Song: "Jonathan, even I doubted our team's success, but you really showed the importance of never giving up and appreciating the journey."

Wenyu Yang

Name: Wenyu Yang
Current Occupation: 3rd Year BME Undergraduate at Johns Hopkins University
Favorite Song: “Save Your Tears” by The Weeknd

I decided to interview my now (and hopefully future) girlfriend Wenyu. I met her online during Zoom university in our first class BME Modeling & Design where we were on the same team. Let it be known here that she chased me 🙂, but jokes aside, Wenyu has really allowed me to experience college besides just hitting Brody on Friday nights.

Last summer, I decided to be foolishly ambitious. I took on a great deal of responsibility. While interning remotely at a pharmaceutical company, I also volunteered at Johns Hopkins Hospital, studied for the MCAT, and made time for my girlfriend. Yep, quite a busy plate. Despite feeling overwhelmed at times, Wenyu reported noticing some of my best moments during this period. I successfully managed my time to prioritize my tasks effectively. I always communicated with my internship manager and was able to develop a fully working database even though I never once touched SQL (a database management software) or backend work. And through the chaos, I also made sure to have fun and take breaks. I visited Towson and Federal Hill park for the first time. Surprisingly, I even learned how to cook, although initially I scared Wenyu with my knife skills and failed to make instant ramen by accidentally throwing away the salt packet.

And from the 3 month period, Wenyu decided I was at my best was when I made her a birthday meal. Even though her birthday was two-weeks before my MCAT exam and near the deadline for my internship project, I took the time to learn recipies for dishes from her family's province in China (Fujian). I knew she had been craving her mother's cooking all summer, so alothough I sure was not as well-versed as her mother, I wanted to repay Wenyu for her immense support. I made Fujian fried noodles and also oyster pancakes , and while Wenyu said the fried noodles were a bit salty, she greatly appreciated how despite my busy schedule I still took time to go out of my comfort zone to make sure she was happy!

Takeaways

So from talking to Mrs. Song and Wenyu, I see that regardless of before and after starting University, I still manage to perform best under pressure. This may be thanks to my (sometimes foolish) optimism. One of my favorite quotes I always tell friends is that "Diamonds are formed under pressure!", and I suppose this does ring true for me. During times of stress, I like to crack jokes and lighten the mood. It does not sit well with me to complain or flaunt how stressed I am, but rather try to reframe how I view issues. For example, even if it's 2 AM and I'm finishing up a project report, instead of honing in on how my back aches, I'd much rather focus on how rewarding it is to be learning and working hard. Appreciating that I have access to opportunities that other people might not have access to pushes me to always be grateful for what I have gone and will go through in life!

Self-Reflection

In this section I reflected upon two personal assessments ( Conflict Management Styles Assessment and 16 Personalities Assessment ) and also a few times I exhibited qualities of transformational leadership. I decided to focus on Transformational Leadership as that was very recently covered in class!

Conflict Management Styles Assessment

Based on the conflict management style assessment, I am a teddy bear (accommodating) who values relationships with others over my own goals. There are 5 possible conflict management styles, each with a maximum score of 12. I scored in the ranges of 4-6 for the other 4 styles and 10 for the accommodating style. So in other words, the accommodating style was pretty dominant. I believe this is relatively accurate as I do value my relationships with friends, mentors, and family and tend to avoid conflicts by just smoothing them over and going back to focus on other tasks. For example, sometimes I will fight with my girlfriend over doing the chores. Our guidelines are whoever does not cook has to wash the dishes, and she usually is the one that cooks as I am not a great cook. In addition, she is a clean freak and dislikes mess in the kitchen. I consider myself not to be messy, but sometimes I leave the dishes off until after I finish my work. Though when I have mountains of work that are due soon, this situation escalates as she rushes me to clean up the dishes. I feel like a knot in the center of the rope pulled in a game of tug of war. In the end, I give in and wash the dishes as she wishes while stressing about my homework. It can be hard at times, trying to keep everyone and myself happy. Most relationships should have some give and take, and I believe I can become more like an owl in the future. I should value not only my relationships but also my desires and goals. I want to to treat conflicts as a way to get closer to the other person, realize our differences, and ease the tension by compromising on both sides. I need to work on my avoidance of conflicts and be brave enough to start the conversion for a resolution.

16 Personalities Assessment

Based on my 16 personality assessment, my personality type is an Assertive Protoganist (ENFJ-A). But when looking at my actual percentages of Extraversion v.s. Intraversion, Intuitive v.s. Observant, Thinking v.s. Feeling, and Judging v.s. Prospecting I found it quite interesting that neither actually had a significant majority. They were all in the range of 52% - 56%. So when the assessment reports that I am Extraverted, I have to think to myself, "Am I really Extraverted?". I showed my lab mentor the results and she was surprised to find I was "Extraverted".

But when looking at the actual percentages, they internally make sense. For example, I may not be 100% Extraverted, but I sure am more Extraverted than I am Intraverted. If there's something I find issues with, I'm more than happy to stand up for what I think. I believe this also plays into my Assertive rating of 64%. If someone is rude towards me or the people I care about, I'm not afraid to speak up. For example, if someone cuts me or my friends in a long line, I'm often the one calling that person out, even if my friends shrug it off. But still, I'm not Extraverted to the point where I seek social connections every hour of the day!

For my 56% Intuitive rating, I find that it's quite accurate!

I role as a diplomat could really help with the previous reflection for my Conflict Management Style Assessment. The personality assessment reports that “Diplomats focus on empathy and cooperation, shining in diplomacy and counseling…often playing the role of harmonizers”. I oftentimes give up my desires to harmonize with others and keep the relationship well. My feeling level is at 53%, meaning I focus on other's emotions, sometimes to the point where I suppress my own emotions. Therefore, perhaps I need to apply the feeling mechanism to myself and reflect introspectively on what I am experiencing and how I can resolve the tension in my own desires. In addition, I will work on increasing my assertiveness which is at 64%. I need to assert my own goals and desires to the other person in a way that would not escalate the conflict. I can apply my empathetic nature and tell people that I am not against them, but working with them to resolve the conflict. In the above example of washing dishes, I could have been more assertive rather than give into my girlfriend. I could have gently talked to my girlfriend about how stressed I was personally about getting my work done before the deadline and how the assignment was more time urgent than the dishes. I could have compromised with her, giving her a hard deadline, say in an hour, when I will go wash the dishes. This would have reduced her anxiety about the dishes not being washed until tomorrow morning and given me time to finish my assignment without stressing as much.

Transformational Leadership

Transformational Leadership is a leadership style that is built upon four pillars: Idealized Influence, Inspirational Motivation, Intellectual Stimulation, and Individualized Consideration [1]. The first pillar of transformational leadership, Idealized Influence, refers to the leader's ability to act as a role model for their followers. This involves demonstrating high ethical standards, setting an example of excellence, and building trust with followers. The second pillar, Inspirational Motivation, involves inspiring and motivating followers to reach their full potential. Transformational leaders do this by communicating a compelling vision, setting challenging goals, and providing a sense of purpose and meaning to their work. The third pillar, Intellectual Stimulation, involves encouraging creativity and innovation within the organization. Transformational leaders challenge their followers to think critically, question assumptions, and seek out new solutions to problems. The fourth and final pillar of Transformational Leadership is Individualized Consideration. This involves showing empathy and concern for the needs and aspirations of individual followers. Transformational leaders invest in the personal and professional growth of their followers, providing support, mentorship, and development opportunities. By combining these four pillars, transformational leaders can inspire and motivate their followers to achieve great things. They provide a clear vision and purpose, act as role models, foster creativity and innovation, and invest in the personal and professional growth of their followers. This approach can lead to positive change and success within the organization or group.

Transformational Leadership is a leadership style that aims to inspire and motivate followers to achieve their full potential, both individually and as a team, in order to bring about positive change. It is a theory that focuses on the followers, their motivations, and their development, rather than just the leader's actions. The idea of transformational leadership was first introduced by James MacGregor Burns in 1978 and expanded upon by Bernard Bass in the 1980s.

One of the key nuances of transformational leadership is that it takes time. This style of leadership is not something that can be implemented overnight. It requires a deep understanding of the needs, aspirations, and strengths of the people you are leading, as well as a willingness to invest the necessary time and effort to develop them. It involves building relationships with followers, gaining their trust, and working with them to set and achieve goals. Another important aspect of Transformational Leadership is that it can be used in conjunction with Transactional Leadership. Transactional Leadership is a more traditional style of leadership that focuses on the exchange of rewards and punishments for performance. While Transformational Leadership aims to inspire and motivate followers, Transactional Leadership aims to provide structure and ensure that tasks are completed efficiently. Combining these two styles can create a well-rounded leadership approach that addresses both the needs of the followers and the organization.

Lastly, Transformational Leadership is about helping people to reach a new level based on their motivations, not necessarily the leader's motivations. This involves understanding what drives each individual, what their goals are, and what obstacles they face. The leader's role is to provide guidance, support, and resources to help followers overcome these obstacles and achieve their goals. By doing so, transformational leaders create a culture of growth and development, where everyone feels valued and supported.

Experience #1: Young Engineers in Action

Space has always fascinated me. The vast unknown scares some, but for me, it is inspiring. I suppose you could guess Interstellar is my favorite movie of all time. Hence, when I was offered the opportunity to start a rocketry team in my local community, I was more than stoked. Being able to engineer a model rocket with 3D printing and electronics sounded too cool to be true. Now as the team was open to every student in the local community, my team was comprised of elementary schoolers to high schoolers. Everyone had different skill sets, knowledge, and interests, and so I looked to meet everyone where they were. For the elementary and middle school members, I held sessions outlining aerodynamic concepts such as drag and propulsion. For those interested in rapid prototyping, I organized CAD workshops. We even had different nationalities on the team, and sometimes I would have to use other languages to communicate my ideas better (Spanish and Chinese). Looking back, I applied the Individualized Consideration aspect of Transformational Leadership, as I provided individualized coaching, mentoring, and support to help my team members achieve their goals and develop their skills.

Experience #2: Charm City Science League

Science Olympiad was a major part of my high school experience, so coming to university I decided to join Charm City Science League (CCSL), a group on campus looking to utilize Science Olympiad to motivate Baltimore students to be interested in STEM. Now serving on the Executive Board, I lead the initiative Build It, Study It (BISI), which is a day event where Baltimore middle school students come to Homewood and go through a science fair learning diverse science concepts! As the director, I believe I embodied Transformational Leadership. I demonstrated Idealized Influence by acting as a role model for the other 30+ volunteers and event planners. My passion for Science Olympiad and STEM education inspired the team to work towards the common goal of promoting interest in science among Baltimore students. I also showed Inspirational Motivation by communicating a compelling vision and purpose for the BISI to the entire leadership and organizing team. While others were worried about potentially being overly-ambitious, I emphasized the importance of exposing students to diverse science concepts, eventually motivating the team to organize 15 unique activities. Furthermore, I demonstrated Intellectual Stimulation by encouraging creativity and innovation within the BISI event. By promoting a wide range of science concepts and encouraging experimentation, I was able to foster a culture of innovation and learning among the participating students. I also provided the organizing team largely unrestrained guidelines to give them as much room possible to be creative about the events they wanted to host for the students.
