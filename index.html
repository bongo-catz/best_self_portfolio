Interviews

I had the pleasure of interviewing two people about a time when I was at my best self. Wanting to see how I changed from high school to university, I decided to interview one person from before and another person from after I entered University.

Mrs. Song

Name: Mrs. Song
Current Occupation: Current High School Biology Teacher at Whitney High School
Favorite Song: "Can't Help Falling in Love" by Elvis Presley

Throughout my time in high school, I knew Mrs. Song well, having first met her in 7th grade. Physically, I didn't change much over the years, but Mrs. Song never failed to notice the personal growth I underwent. From a shy yet studious student who consistently aced Kahoot quizzes, I matured into a sociable and people-oriented leader. In my sophomore year, I became involved in Mrs. Song's effort to establish a Science Olympiad program in our school. For three years, we worked tirelessly to build and hone our team.

In my senior year, Mrs. Song and I were determined to see our team advance to the States Competition. Unfortunately, on the day of the Regionals Competition, everything seemed to go wrong. Our bus arrived late, and then we were stuck in traffic. We even missed out on competing in two events. Despite the setbacks, I maintained a positive attitude and encouraged my teammates to keep going. I reminded them how much we had practiced together and tried to lift their spirits by emphasizing the importance of having fun.

After six grueling hours of competition, we finally made it to the awards ceremony. As they announced the placements of the teams, I watched anxiously, hoping for the best. Though as 6th through 3rd place were announced, I saw Mrs. Song shaking her head in disappointment. But I refused to give up hope and exclaimed “Hey, there are still 2 places left.” And then it happened - our team was declared the second-place winner!

Mrs. Song recounted this story to share that I was at my best when I utilized my optimism to help lift my teammates up, even when everything seemed to go wrong. When I turned to my team during the awards ceremony and said that there were still 2 places left, Mrs. Song felt it showed how much I believed in the people I cared for. While others were becoming discouraged again, I still maintained hope. A quote from Mrs. Song: "Jonathan, even I doubted our team's success, but you really showed the importance of never giving up and appreciating the journey."

Takeaways

So from talking to Mrs. Song and Wenyu, I see that regardless of before and after starting University, I still manage to perform best under pressure. This may be thanks to my (sometimes foolish) optimism. One of my favorite quotes I always tell friends is that "Diamonds are formed under pressure!", and I suppose this does ring true for me. During times of stress, I like to crack jokes and lighten the mood. It does not sit well with me to complain or flaunt how stressed I am, but rather try to reframe how I view issues. Self-Reflection

In this section I dive deeper into Transformational Leadership and also a few times I exhibited qualities of this leadership principle. I also reflected upon two personal assessments ( Conflict Management Styles Assessment and 16 Personalities Assessment )

Transformational Leadership

Transformational Leadership is a leadership style that is built upon four pillars: Idealized Influence, Inspirational Motivation, Intellectual Stimulation, and Individualized Consideration [1]. The first pillar of Transformational Leadership, Idealized Influence, refers to the leader's ability to act as a role model for their followers. This involves demonstrating high ethical standards, setting an example of excellence, and building trust with followers. The second pillar, Inspirational Motivation, involves inspiring and motivating followers to reach their full potential. Transformational leaders do this by communicating a compelling vision, setting challenging goals, and providing a sense of purpose and meaning to their work. The third pillar, Intellectual Stimulation, involves encouraging creativity and innovation within the organization. Transformational leaders challenge their followers to think critically, question assumptions, and seek out new solutions to problems. The fourth and final pillar of Transformational Leadership is Individualized Consideration. This involves showing empathy and concern for the needs and aspirations of individual followers. Transformational leaders invest in the personal and professional growth of their followers, providing support, mentorship, and development opportunities. By combining these four pillars, Transformational leaders can inspire and motivate their followers to achieve great things. They provide a clear vision and purpose, act as role models, foster creativity and innovation, and invest in the personal and professional growth of their followers. This approach can lead to positive change and success within the organization or group.

Transformational Leadership is a leadership style that aims to inspire and motivate followers to achieve their full potential, both individually and as a team, in order to bring about positive change. It is a theory that focuses on the followers, their motivations, and their development, rather than just the leader's actions. The idea of Transformational Leadership was first introduced by James MacGregor Burns in 1978 and expanded upon by Bernard Bass in the 1980s [2].

One of the key nuances of Transformational Leadership is that it takes time [2]. This style of leadership is not something that can be implemented overnigh. It requires a deep understanding of the needs, aspirations, and strengths of the people you are leading, as well as a willingness to invest the necessary time and effort to develop them. It involves building relationships with followers, gaining their trust, and working with them to set and achieve goals. Another important aspect of Transformational Leadership is that it can be used in conjunction with Transactional Leadership. Transactional Leadership is a more traditional style of leadership that focuses on the exchange of rewards and punishments for performance. While Transformational Leadership aims to inspire and motivate followers, Transactional Leadership aims to provide structure and ensure that tasks are completed efficiently. Combining these two styles can create a well-rounded leadership approach that addresses both the needs of the followers and the organization.

Lastly, Transformational Leadership is about helping people to reach a new level based on their motivations, not necessarily the leader's motivations [2]. This involves understanding what drives each individual, what their goals are, and what obstacles they face. The leader's role is to provide guidance, support, and resources to help followers overcome these obstacles and achieve their goals. By doing so, Transformational leaders create a culture of growth and development, where everyone feels valued and supported.

Experience #1: Young Engineers in Action

Space has always fascinated me. The vast unknown scares some, but for me, it is inspiring. I suppose you could guess Interstellar is my favorite movie of all time. Hence, when I was offered the opportunity to start a rocketry team in my local community, I was more than stoked. Being able to engineer a model rocket with 3D printing and electronics sounded too cool to be true. Now as the team was open to every student in the local community, my team was comprised of elementary schoolers to high schoolers. Everyone had different skill sets, knowledge, and interests, and so I looked to meet everyone where they were. For the elementary and middle school members, I held sessions outlining aerodynamic concepts such as drag and propulsion. For those interested in rapid prototyping, I organized CAD workshops. We even had different nationalities on the team, and sometimes I would have to use other languages to communicate my ideas better (Spanish and Chinese). Looking back, I applied the Individualized Consideration aspect of Transformational Leadership, as I provided individualized coaching, mentoring, and support to help my team members achieve their goals and develop their skills.

Experience #2: Charm City Science League

Science Olympiad was a major part of my high school experience, so coming to university I decided to join Charm City Science League (CCSL), a group on campus looking to utilize Science Olympiad to motivate Baltimore students to be interested in STEM. Now serving on the Executive Board, I lead the initiative Build It, Study It (BISI), which is a day event where Baltimore middle school students come to Homewood and go through a science fair learning diverse science concepts! As the director, I believe I embodied Transformational Leadership. I demonstrated Idealized Influence by acting as a role model for the other 30+ volunteers and event planners. My passion for Science Olympiad and STEM education inspired the team to work towards the common goal of promoting interest in science among Baltimore students. I also showed Inspirational Motivation by communicating a compelling vision and purpose for the BISI to the entire leadership and organizing team. While others were worried about potentially being overly-ambitious, I emphasized the importance of exposing students to diverse science concepts, eventually motivating the team to organize 15 unique activities. Furthermore, I demonstrated Intellectual Stimulation by encouraging creativity and innovation within the BISI event. By promoting a wide range of science concepts and encouraging experimentation, I was able to foster a culture of innovation and learning among the participating students. I also provided the organizing team largely unrestrained guidelines to give them as much room possible to be creative about the events they wanted to host for the students.

Conflict Management Styles Assessment

Based on the conflict management style assessment, I am a teddy bear (accommodating) who values relationships with others over my own goals. There are 5 possible conflict management styles, each with a maximum score of 12. I scored in the ranges of 4-6 for the other 4 styles and 10 for the accommodating style. So in other words, the accommodating style was pretty dominant. I believe this is relatively accurate as I do value my relationships with friends, mentors, and family and tend to avoid conflicts by just smoothing them over and going back to focus on other tasks. For example, sometimes I will fight with my girlfriend over doing the chores. Our guidelines are whoever does not cook has to wash the dishes, and she usually is the one that cooks as I am not a great cook. In addition, she is a clean freak and dislikes mess in the kitchen. I consider myself not to be messy, but sometimes I leave the dishes off until after I finish my work. Though when I have mountains of work that are due soon, this situation escalates as she rushes me to clean up the dishes. I feel like a knot in the center of the rope pulled in a game of tug of war. In the end, I give in and wash the dishes as she wishes while stressing about my homework. It can be hard at times, trying to keep everyone and myself happy. Most relationships should have some give and take, and I believe I can become more like an owl in the future. I should value not only my relationships but also my desires and goals. I want to to treat conflicts as a way to get closer to the other person, realize our differences, and ease the tension by compromising on both sides. I need to work on my avoidance of conflicts and be brave enough to start the conversion for a resolution.

16 Personalities Assessment

Based on my 16 personality assessment, my personality type is an Assertive Protoganist (ENFJ-A). Though when analyzing my actual percentages of Extraversion v.s. Intraversion, Intuitive v.s. Observant, Thinking v.s. Feeling, and Judging v.s. Prospecting I found it quite interesting that neither actually had a significant majority. They were all in the range of 52% - 56%. So when the assessment reports that I am Extraverted, I have to think to myself, "Am I really Extraverted?". I showed my lab mentor the results and even she was surprised to find I was "Extraverted".

But the percentages do internally make sense. For example, I may not be 100% Extraverted, but I sure am more Extraverted than I am Intraverted. If there's something I find issues with, I'm more than happy to stand up for what I think. I believe this also plays into my Assertive rating of 64%. If someone is rude towards me or the people I care about, I'm not afraid to speak up. For example, if someone cuts me or my friends in a long line, I'm often the one calling that person out, even if my friends shrug it off. But still, I'm not Extraverted to the point where I seek social connections every hour of the day!

For my 56% Intuitive rating, I find that I do often lean towards the more visionary side of planning. Back to my CCSL example with planning BISI, I laid out the goal of the event to offer diverse events for students to experience and learn from! And when it comes to prototyping and designing projects, I often like to look back upon not only my previous experiences, but also the efforts of past researchers and engineers. Back in my high school robotics team, I would be the one members always throwing out outlandish ideas, hoping they stick. You can find such an example in our robot we designed for the 2015 season, where I proposed building an expanding robot that we called "Rex" (it looks like a T-Rex).

Having a 53% Feeling rating was quite surprising as I think of myself as generally less emotionally aware than most. I would say I prefer using objective facts to make decisions (as an engineer), but after reading more about Thinking v.s. Feeling, I can't lie, it does make sense. When I thought about my past decisions more, I think about how often I choose to follow my gut feeling. As I have matured, I have found that the decisions I have to make usually have less and less "right answers". There's no objectively "best" decision. Take last summer for example, where I almost ran myself dry with commitments. Objectively speaking, I should not have tried piling so much on my plate, but my heart pushed me to explore new experiences and to not have any regrets. Or even riding roller coasters! When I was younger, I absolutely hated going to amusement parks (I'm looking at you Six Flags). Today though, I love roller coasters. Objectively speaking, there's no need to put my life at risk for a few minutes of thrill, but my heart pushes me to take risks. I do also try to consider other people around me. After a late night at Brody, I ran into a fellow CCSL Executive Board Member Elaine who said she was heading to FFU. Noting that she was going to walk alone and that it was nearly 10:00 PM, I decided to accompany Elaine to FFU to make sure she reached her destination safely.

The 53% Judging rating was also another surprising one. I initially thought I was more of a "free-flowing" planner described as the Prospecting trait. But when push comes to shove, I do much rather prefer to have a plan in mind. While I hate figuring out logistics, what I hate more is not knowing what the hell is going to happen. So even if I do procrastinate on planning events (trips, team meetings, schedules), I notice I do eventually draft up several plans. Just this past week when I visited my sister in San Francisco, while my dad wanted a "chill" trip with no set plan or schedules, I still found myself laying out what places we were all going to visit. I even laid out what times expected to be spent at each location.

Now for Assertive v.s. Turbulent, I had a 64% rating for Assertive, which was my highest rating. Scrolling down the page explaning Assertive v.s. Turbulent, I will say the quote "Assertive personality types learn lessons from their personal histories, but they see little purpose in entertaining the same regrets again and again" truly spoke to me. I learn from lessons in my past instead of trying to have repeats of mistakes. During my summer internship at a pharmaceutical company, I was assigned the responsibility of building a remote database application. And while I was excited to dive in and start coding, I quickly realized that I didn't fully understand the project requirements or the underlying technologies. Rather than admitting my lack of understanding and seeking help, I tried to power through and code my way to a solution. However, my code ended up being messy and buggy, and my team members had to spend extra time fixing my mistakes. To learn from my mistake, I realized that I needed to prioritize learning and understanding the project requirements and technologies before jumping into coding. I started asking questions and seeking help from more experienced team members, and I spent time researching and studying the underlying technologies. I now know to always understand the problem first before trying to implement a solution. The other qualities of being Assertive, such as being self-assured and even-tempered, is aligned with my optimism when faced with stress. I don't just focus on the goal, but also look to appreciate the journey and hope to grow whenever possible.

Growth

Often in leadership positions, I don't really think of myself different from the other members. I rather approach it as if I am one of them, similar to how in Situational Leadership S3 leaders act in a supporting role. The leader often acts as an "equal" team member embedded within the team [1]. I appreciate this form because I really value giving other members platforms to share their ideas and express their opinions. But sometimes I find it hard to be aggressive or demanding of other members, especially when it comes to setting deadlines and making sure the team moves forward. I think the best example of this could be with my Clark Scholar Engineering Design class.

Clark Scholar Engineering Challenge

Some context: This is a 1-credit class where we are currently looking to help fight the opioid crisis in Baltimore. We are looking to prototype a solution to increase access to harm-reduction resources (Naloxone, clean needles, counseling, etc.). Our group has been a part of this project starting in the Spring of 2022. The

Issue: Given that we are in a 1-credit class and as students balance 15+ credits from other classes, progress from this project has been a bit slow. While we are all motivated about the project, sometimes we just get overwhelmed with the other responsibilities in our lives and miss important project deadlines.

Immediate Improvements: As a part of project management, I will look to implement a formal schedule with dependencies, deliverables, and phases. I met with Danica Mackesey, a project manager at Key Technologies to better understand the role, and learned that being in the position means constantly checking in on members and sometimes micromanaging. When I heard this, I immediately noted how it went against my mode of being "hands-off", but Danica stressed that project management involves finding a balance between being hands-off and micromanaging. While it is important to trust your team and give them autonomy, it is also crucial to monitor progress and ensure that everyone is on the same page. As a project manager, one must be able to identify when to step in and provide guidance or support, and when to step back and let the team take the lead.

Having a formal schedule with dependencies, deliverables, and phases can help with this balancing act, as it provides a clear roadmap for the project and allows us to monitor progress and identify potential issues early on. However, it is also important to maintain open lines of communication with the team and encourage them to speak up if they need help or have concerns.

Being Held Accountable: Another team member Jillayne is a backup project manager on the team and will help to make sure I keep in communication with the team on deadlines and deliverables. I have promised to meet with every sub-team at least twice a week and Jillayne will follow up with me to make sure I have completed those meetings. Jillayne will also check to see that I have scheduled design reviews every three weeks with our community partners to make sure our team is getting feedback as needed during prototyping.

Intended Impact: Having a clear schedule will hopefully help in the following ways.

  1. Provides clarity and direction: A clear schedule gives team members a clear understanding of what is expected of them and when. This helps to reduce confusion and ambiguity and ensures that everyone is working towards the same goals. For example, with Evan and Bharath working on the backend and Mariah and Andrew working on the frontend, a schedule will help them see what the two interrelated subteams are working on. If Evan and Bharath are thinking of incorporating a new database, then Mariah and Andrew can simultaneously work on new frontend features to incorporate.
  2. Helps with planning and prioritization: With a clear schedule, team members can better plan their work and prioritize their tasks. They can see how their work fits into the overall project and can adjust their priorities to ensure that they are meeting the project's deadlines and milestones.
  3. Facilitates collaboration and communication: A clear schedule can help facilitate collaboration and communication among team members. They can see how their work overlaps with others and can coordinate their efforts to avoid duplication or conflicts.
  4. Improves accountability: A clear schedule helps to improve accountability by establishing clear expectations and deadlines. Team members are more likely to take responsibility for their work when they have clear goals and deadlines to meet.
